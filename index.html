    Conway's Game of Life!

    The simple code in the page allows you to try out what a one-dimensional version of Conway's Game of Life would be like. Except you pick your own rules. Each cell is either alive or dead (on/off, 1/0, High/low), and its transition will depend on whether its neighbours are alive/dead.

    At every period, all cells will move to their next state, producing interesting patterns.

    Play with it and see what you can make :)

    About the Game of Life

    The Game of Life is particularly well known among geeks. It's a zero-player game, where once the initial conditions are set, the destiny of all things will naturally unfold.
    And the player does nothing about it... well, except set the initial conditions.

    This seemingly trivial concept was invented by no other than John von Neunmann who was thinking about self-replicating robots. These would have to go through 26 states of construction before finishing their replication.
    When he realized the complexity of the task, he sort-of gave up.
    Instead, he took up the work of one of his colleges in crystal growth, as crystals grow outwards in a periodic pattern, they were far-simplified analogues of replicating robots (also in some repeating pattern).
    The different possible molecules mapped to the different possible states.
    The final result of their work was Cellular Automata, the idea that individual "cells" would be affected by their immediate neighbours to change state.

    Then Conway came along and popularized the concept with The Game Of Life.
    A simple set of rules resulting in dynamic and complex behaviours.

    The last brilliant player in the discovery of this construct was Stephen Wolfram.
    He proved that the simples for of this construct, a one-dimensional line of 'cells' were capable of being programmed...
    To do anything. Anything at all.
    This property known as Turing Completeness makes for a very mind-blowing concept.
    8 simple rules are enough to construct a Turing complete machine... (with a little exponential overhead, of course)

That means that they can be used to "silence" gene expression. They are, however, tediously difficult and meticulous to design.
